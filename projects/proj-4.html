Spring 2016

Technologies: Python, TensorFlow
Key Words: genomics, epigenetics, recurrent neural network, multitask learning

The last course I took for my Master's degree was Deep Learning for Natural Language Processing taught by Richard Socher. Working with a fellow member of my research group, we speculated that the deep learning models designed for sequential data such as natural language could naturally be applied to genomic sequences. For our final project, we first confirmed that a character-level recurrent neural network can capture the non-random parts of DNA by comparing the perplexity obtained after training on a real genome to that obtained after training on a random sequence of nucleotides. We then trained a bidirectional character-level RNN to predict whether a given genomic sequence would interact with a variety of transcription factors, DNase I hypersensitive sites, and histone marks. Because multiple biological objects can interact with a given sequence, we casted this latter problem as a multitask learning problem. We empirically showed how a deep network could outperform a baseline logistic regression model on a significant majority of 919 binary labeling tasks. We used epigenetic data from the ENCODE and Roadmap Epigenomics Mapping consortia. Please see our writeup for more details. The code is also available on Github.
